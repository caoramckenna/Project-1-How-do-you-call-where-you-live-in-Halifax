How do you call the place that you live?

The ways we refer to where we live often depends on the context of who we're speaking with. By Caora McKenna.

When I'm telling people from around the world where I live, I usually tell them I live in Canada.

Then, if they're familiar and interested, I'll say that I live in Nova Scotia, on the east coast of Canada.

Next, I'll usually tell people I live in Halifax, which sometimes registers more than Nova Scotia does.

I often add that I live on the peninsula, because Halifax the municipality is massive and sprawls along the Atlantic coast from Peggy's Cove to Ecum Secum.


If the context changes from meeting new people to selling a pair of pants to someone on Facebook Marketplace, I usually say I live near the Halifax Common.

And add that I'm on Charles Street between Robie and Windsor Streets, because I want them to get the impression that I'm easy to get to, so they're more likely to come and buy the pants.

I was thinking about all this because I was inspired by this map of New York City neighbourhoods as defined by their residents..

So I asked some of my friends, old coworkers and hockey teammates to tell me where they live and how they call it. Everyone who responded lives within a 20 minute drive from my house, but among the 30 people who answered, there were over 10 different ways people referred to the area where they lived.

Everyone responded with their own imagined context for why I would be asking them where they live and how much I know myself about the areas of Halifax. One friend, for example, said they call the place where they live "Flower Streets." They knew I was familiar with Dartmouth, and so likely to understand the reference. Another friend said "Larry Uteck," which I knew was a road that snakes from the Bedford Highway to the 102, but might have referred to as Bedford if someone asked me.

In case you, like me, didn't know, Larry Uteck was a Canadian Football Hall of famer, who also served as the Deputy Mayor of Halifax in 1998.
